03
The model debate

Account-based pods
or offering-based pods?

The instinct to build autonomous, self-directed teams that operate like mini-agencies is exactly right. The question is what we organize them around. Account-based pods are built around who we serve. Offering-based pods are built around what we deliver. One is fragile. One compounds.

Client-based pods
Built around who you serve, not what you deliver
You cannot size a pod around clients you cannot control. Client A churns at month 8. Client B doubles scope. Every client event forces a restructure.
Generalist pods produce generalist work. A designer doing web, social, and pitch decks never develops mastery in any of them.
Career paths collapse. One designer per pod means one ceiling per pod — a generalist MD above, not a craft leader.
Knowledge is trapped in the pod MD. When they leave, client context leaves with them.
Innovation scatters. Pod A builds differently from Pod B. Nothing compounds. The wheel is rebuilt every time.
"Pods focused around accounts/clients are nearly impossible to sustain because of the ever-changing nature of clients." — Sammy, Vision Exercise
Outcome-based offering teams
Built around what you deliver, not who you serve
The Web Growth team's size is set by how much web work exists, not by which clients are in it. Clients change. The offering is stable.
Deep specialization is enforced by structure. The social creative on the Social Growth team makes only social creative, every day. Mastery follows.
Career paths multiply. Every offering has its own CD, its own Director. Four people can hold those titles simultaneously without anyone being wrong.
Client knowledge is distributed. The AM owns the relationship. The Offering Lead owns delivery. Either can change without disrupting the other.
Collective intelligence compounds. The Web Growth team builds every website. Their frameworks and standards get better with every engagement.
The offering pod IS Bin's vision — correctly structured. Autonomous teams, strong leads, operating independently. Same idea. Much stronger foundation.

05
The most important new role

The Offering Lead.
It changes everything.

This role does not exist today. It is the hinge on which the entire model turns. Every current pain point — scope creep, misaligned traffic, AM overload, burnout — has a structural solution in this role.

OFFERING LEAD
Senior-most person in each outcome team. Does not exist today.
Owns
Quality standard, internal staffing on each engagement, innovation mandate, team utilization
Receives from
Traffic Manager (routing) and AM (structured brief) — not ad hoc Slack messages
Does NOT do
Client relationship management, financial conversations, scope negotiations — those stay with the AM
Accountable for
The quality standard, innovation roadmap, delivery excellence, and client expectations within their offering — the person who owns how that discipline performs across every engagement
It solves Mapi's traffic problem
Mapi said traffic managers are "assigning blindly" because they don't understand creative workflows. The traffic manager routes to the Offering Lead — who then staffs internally. The traffic manager doesn't need to understand the work. The Offering Lead does.
It removes the AM from delivery management
Currently AMs manage deliverables because there's no one else. The Offering Lead takes delivery off the AM's plate entirely. AM briefs the Offering Lead. Offering Lead runs the engagement. AM manages the client relationship and growth roadmap.
It makes scope visible before it drifts
The Offering Lead owns hours and scope for their team's engagements. They flag capacity concerns before the AM or client feels the impact. Megan's vision — "no surprise scope creep because it is tracked" — is realized through this role.
Eight offerings. Eight leadership paths.
Eight offerings means eight Offering Lead roles. Eight paths to Director. Eight paths to VP. Every specialist now has a leadership track inside their discipline — not one narrow path at the top of the whole company.

Questions worth asking

Possible
concerns.
Honest answers.

Any time a model changes, the same questions come up. These are the most common ones — answered directly.

On specialization
"Does moving to offering teams mean I'm locked into one lane forever?"
The opposite. Right now, most people are forced to be generalists because the structure requires it. In the offering model, specialization is what creates the career path. The deeper you go in your offering, the more leadership opportunities open up — lead, director, VP — all within a discipline you actually want to master. You're not locked in. You're finally given room to go deep.
On quality standards
"If teams are split across offerings, won't quality get inconsistent?"
Quality gets inconsistent today because there is no single owner of it. In the outcome model, each Offering Lead owns the quality standard for their team — full stop. They set the bar, hire to it, and are accountable for every engagement that comes out of their team. One standard, enforced structurally. That is more consistent than today, not less.
On team continuity
"What happens to the teams we've already built? Do we start over?"
No team gets dismantled. The shift is about naming what already exists and giving it a proper structure. Teams that are already working together on a type of work become the foundation of that offering team. The offering model doesn't ask us to rebuild from scratch — it asks us to stop pretending the current org chart reflects how work actually flows. It mostly doesn't.
On change itself
"This is a big structural shift. What if we get it wrong?"
The bigger risk is staying in a model that everyone — independently, without comparing notes — described as unsustainable. The offering model is not a gamble. It is a response to what the team already said it needs. We don't have to flip everything at once. But we do have to start building toward it deliberately, because the current structure cannot scale to where we all said we want to go.
