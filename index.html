Response Analysis Team Archetypes Offering Lead AI Playbook 50M Roadmap
Marketwake · Leadership Vision · 2026

ONE HOUSE.
ONE DIRECTION.
ONE STANDARD.

Everyone submitted their 3 and 5 year plans. What came back was not ten different futures — while there were differences in the how, nearly all of us described a very similar future. These maps show that picture. The orange dot is where we are going together. Every line is momentum.

Hover any dot to read what that person said. The orange ring is the shared organizational target.
01
One House
One standard, one voice, one direction — regardless of which team a client touches or which offering they buy
02
Architects, not doers
Every role evolves from execution toward design — building the systems, frameworks, and standards that scale without heroic effort
03
AI in every function
AI fluency is not a specialty — it is a baseline expectation across every role, every team, every offering
04
Control what we control
Delivery excellence, process quality, team structure, and client experience — these are ours to own completely
Map 01 · The transition

From doing
to architecting

This map shows where each leader sits on two spectrums: how channel-specific vs. cross-functional their current role is, and how much they are still personally executing vs. directing and designing.

What the pattern tells us: The entire team is in motion toward the same upper-right corner — away from execution and toward architecture. The target isn't to stop caring about craft. It's to stop being the only person who can do it. The architect sets the standard. The team executes it.

This is the One House principle in organizational form: when every leader designs systems instead of filling gaps, the quality becomes structural — not dependent on who happens to be available.

X: Channel specialist → Cross-functional  |  Y: Hands-on executor → Director / Architect  |  Orange ring = One House target
The AI connection
AI enables the transition
The path from doer to architect is accelerated when AI absorbs the repeatable execution layer. Every role that learns to delegate to AI gains the bandwidth to design the system around it. This is not a future state — it starts now.
What to build toward
Systems that run without heroic effort
Megan wrote it directly: "Systems must scale without heroic effort." The architect's job is to make the system so good that the individual effort becomes invisible. Every offering team needs this as its standard.
Map 02 · The offering shift

Selling outcomes,
not channels

This map plots how outcome-oriented each person's vision is, and how boldly they described moving away from work that doesn't serve that future.

What the pattern tells us: Every person independently described the same shift — from channel-based delivery to outcome-based growth systems. Nobody was told to say this. The team already believes it. What we're building together is the structure that makes it real.

The eight offering teams — Web Growth, Brand & Creative, Social Growth, Performance, Content & Organic Authority, Video & Production, Lifecycle & Email, AI & Intelligence — are the organizational form of what everyone described wanting.

X: Channel-centric vision → Outcome-centric vision  |  Y: Incremental evolution → Bold exit from current work
The unanimous signal
10 of 10 said the same thing
Every single team member — across every discipline — described moving from channel execution to outcome ownership. This is the strongest signal in the entire exercise. The direction is not in question. Only the pace is.
What Sammy described
"Growth systems that compound"
"We are designing growth systems that compound over time — not managing isolated channels." This is the offering model in one sentence. The system compounds. The channel depletes.
What this means for delivery
One client experience, regardless of offering
Every client who touches Marketwake — regardless of which offering they buy — should feel the same standard: white glove, strategic, seamless, expert. The offering model makes that possible because every team owns their outcome completely.
Map 03 · The readiness picture

Building with
intention,
not accident

This map shows the balance between how urgently each person wants change and how structurally ready their area is to support it — the core tension between ambition and foundation.

What the pattern tells us: The team's energy is genuine and directional. The work is to sequence correctly — building the operational infrastructure alongside the strategic ambition, so the org moves together rather than some functions racing ahead and others catching up.

This is what "building intentionally, not accidentally" looks like as a map. The target is alignment between urgency and readiness — which is exactly the condition that produces smooth, scalable delivery.

X: Needs more runway → Structurally ready  |  Y: Comfortable with pace → High urgency to change
The sequencing principle
Ops infrastructure is the foundation everything else builds on
The target on this map is a diagonal — urgency and readiness moving together. The Ops team building the systems that enable everyone else is not support work. It is the highest-leverage investment in the building right now. Automated proposals, capacity dashboards, structured intake, billing infrastructure — these are the conditions that make every other team's vision achievable.
What "control what we control" means here
Delivery quality is entirely within our control
We cannot control which clients come in, what the market does, or how competitors move. We can completely control how work flows, how quality is enforced, how clients are onboarded, and how outcomes are measured. Every person on this team described wanting that control. The offering model and the Ops infrastructure build are what give it to us.
Maps 04 · 05 · 06

Leadership, influence
& building the new

Three maps that together show what kind of organization Marketwake is becoming: one where leaders develop leaders, where the company's external reputation is built intentionally, and where the team is comfortable moving ahead of proof.

Leadership: The team is shifting from managing output to designing the teams and systems that produce output without constant oversight.

Influence: Marketwake's future growth model — named by 8 of 10 people — is inbound. Clients find us because of what we say and what we publish. Every leader's external voice is a business development asset.

Risk: The offering model requires moving ahead of certainty on a few structural bets — team design, service model, client selection. The team's collective risk appetite, mapped here, shows they are ready for that.

Map 04 · Leadership development
X: Managing output → Building people  |  Y: Developing today → Designing the future team
Map 05 · Personal brand & influence
X: Internal focus → External presence  |  Y: Reactive → Intentional & strategic
Map 06 · Risk & innovation tolerance
X: Optimize existing → Build the new  |  Y: Waits for proof → Moves ahead of certainty
On leadership development
Eight offerings. Eight leadership tracks.
The offering model doesn't just change how we deliver. It multiplies the number of leadership paths in the company. Every offering needs a Lead, a Director, eventually a VP. Those are paths that don't exist today. The map shows the team is already thinking about building them.
On external influence
The content engine as growth strategy
8 of 10 people described future growth coming from inbound — clients who find us because of our reputation, our research, our point of view. That future requires every leader to contribute to the external voice of Marketwake. Not as extra work — as part of the role.
On risk and innovation
The structural bets are already decided
The offering model, the Offering Lead role, the outcome-based ICP — these require moving ahead of proof. The team's risk appetite, mapped here, shows they are ready to make those bets. The work is sequencing and executing them, not debating them.

people described the same future without coordinating
"We are no longer simply managing marketing campaigns. We are designing growth systems that compound over time. Clients view us as their outsourced growth leadership team, not just a marketing vendor."
Sammy · Director of Strategy
Maps 07 · 08

The skills we need
& how we define excellence

These two maps show what the team believes the most critical capabilities are, and how each person defines excellence — drawn directly from their answers to "What standards or behaviors define excellence for your team in this year?"

Skills: Across the board, the team described a shift toward systems thinking, data fluency, AI-enabled operations, and cross-team leadership — away from channel-specific craft as the primary value. AI fluency is the through-line in every response. It appeared in every single vision, not as a tool but as an expectation.

Future talent profile: Where each person currently sits on the spectrum from channel expert to systems thinker, and from reactive executor to proactive builder — and where the future Marketwake needs everyone to be heading.

Map 07 · Critical skills
X: Technical / craft → Strategic / systems  |  Y: Individual contributor → Leadership & cross-team
Map 08 · How we define excellence
X: Process compliance → Judgment & outcomes  |  Y: Individual excellence → Team / cultural excellence
AI as the baseline
Not a specialty. A standard.
AI appeared in every single vision response — not as a department or a tool, but as an operating assumption. Success in every role going forward is measured in part by how effectively that person uses AI to increase leverage, speed, and quality. This is what "AI in every function" looks like as a talent standard.
The skills transition
From craft mastery to systems mastery
The most valued skill across all responses was the same: knowing how channels work together, not just how one channel works. Marketing systems thinking, data storytelling, experimentation design — these are the capabilities the offering model is built to develop and reward.
Who the future Marketwake needs
Curious. Proactive. Systems-minded.
Across multiple responses, the team described the same profile: people who move quickly, take ownership, see AI as a lever, and care about the system as much as the deliverable. The hiring bar and the promotion bar are the same bar — described here by the team themselves.

Excellence &
what success feels like

These final two maps show how the team defines excellence and what signals they believe will confirm that Marketwake is working at the level it wants to reach.

Excellence: This is the tightest cluster of all 11 maps. The team's definition of excellence is more aligned than any other question. Everyone described the same thing in different words: judgment over process, ownership over compliance, creative ambition matched with measurable performance.

Signals: The team largely described the same future success state — inbound clients, expanding retainers, teams running without constant escalation, work that wins awards and attracts talent. These are external and leading, which is exactly right.

Map 09 · Defining excellence

Map 10 · Success signals
X: Internal signals → External signals  |  Y: Lagging indicators → Leading indicators
The biggest cultural strength
The team already agrees on what excellent looks like
Of all 11 maps, this one shows the tightest cluster. No other question produced this level of alignment. The team instinctively agrees that excellence means judgment, ownership, creative bravery, and measurable results — not checklist compliance. The offering model formalizes and scales that instinct. It doesn't create it. This is already who we are. The structure is catching up to us.
What success looks like — in the team's own words
Clients expand without being asked. Talent comes to us.
The signals the team described: clients increase budgets because results are clear. Inbound referrals reference our thought leadership. Teams operate without leadership intervention. The work wins recognition. These are all outputs of the One House model — a single standard, delivered consistently, across every client who encounters us. When every engagement feels white glove, strategic, and seamlessly expert, these signals follow naturally.

Five signals that appeared in every response

01
From channel execution to strategic systems. Every single person described a future where their team no longer does the work of today's execution roles. Specialization over generalism. Nine of ten responses explicitly named the generalist problem. People wearing too many hats. Roles too broad. Work bleeding across undefined ownership. The Year 3 vision is a much more specialized organization where depth beats breadth in every discipline.

Creative and performance must merge. Unanimous frustration with the siloed relationship between creative and performance. 9 of 10
04
AI as infrastructure, not novelty. AI appears in every single response — not as an exciting new tool, but as an assumption. 10 of 10
05

Repeating problems

Five things that kept surfacing unprompted

Scope creep
Named in 7 of 10 responses
Not a client problem. A sales and scoping process problem. The fix is upstream — in how work gets scoped and sold, not in how teams push back after the fact.
Wrong clients
Named in 8 of 10 responses
Eight of ten people described walking away from certain client types as a prerequisite for the future they envision. Client selection is a strategic lever, not just a sales problem.
Burnout
Named in 6 of 10 responses
Framed as a structural problem — wrong clients, wrong scopes, too-generalist roles. The team is not asking for wellness perks. They are describing an organizational design issue.
Silos
Named in 6 of 10 responses
The structural symptom of a company organized by channel rather than by outcome. Everyone describes the same future organization from their own vantage point. The opportunity is to name it explicitly.
Manual work
Named in 8 of 10 responses
Proposals, invoices, reports, audits, briefs — all named as work that should already be automated. These are not Year 3 problems. They are today problems with today solutions.
The biggest opportunity

Becoming known, not just competent

Eight of ten respondents described a future where inbound leads reference Marketwake's thought leadership, frameworks, or reputation. Zero respondents described cold outreach as their future growth path. The team has collectively decided that the path to better clients is a stronger Marketwake brand and a highly intentional sales function.

The compounding investment
Marketwake as a media and creative company that happens to do marketing
Josh's thought leadership frameworks. Kristen's award-winning campaigns. Sydney's culture-driven media company. Mapi's Cannes Lion. G's prestige production. Amber's reputation-driven inbound. None of these are achievable in isolation. All are achievable together if Marketwake invests in its own brand with the same discipline it applies to client work.
Start, stop, decide
What the responses tell us to do today
Start: Internal marketing engine. Ops infrastructure. Company-wide ICP definition. AI integration roadmap. Thought leadership content program.

Stop: Accepting misaligned clients. Manual proposals, invoices, reports. Protecting generalists unwilling to specialize. Siloed channel delivery on integrated clients.

Decide: Pod model vs. outcome offering teams. Social org structure. AM role definition.
